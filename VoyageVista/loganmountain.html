History of Logan Mountain

Rising 3,954 meters (12,972 ft) in the Canadian Rockies, Logan Mountain stands as one of Canada's most iconic peaks. Named after Sir William Logan, founder of the Geological Survey of Canada, the mountain has been sacred to Indigenous peoples for millennia before European explorers arrived in the 19th century.

1858

First recorded sighting by European explorers during the Palliser Expedition. Named after Sir William Logan in 1860.

1925

First successful summit by a Canadian climbing team after three failed attempts.

1965

Included within the newly established Logan Mountain Provincial Park, protecting 542 km¬≤ of wilderness.

1984

Designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Canadian Rocky Mountain Parks.

Present

Receives over 200,000 visitors annually while maintaining strict conservation protocols.

Seasonal Highlights

Spring (May-June)

Wildflower blooms in the alpine meadows, melting snow creates spectacular waterfalls, and wildlife becomes more active after winter.

Summer (July-August)

Ideal for hiking with all trails accessible. Long daylight hours and warm temperatures make this peak season.

Fall (September-October)

Stunning golden larch displays, fewer crowds, and excellent wildlife viewing as animals prepare for winter.

Winter (November-April)

Snow transforms the landscape into a winter wonderland. Cross-country skiing and snowshoeing opportunities abound.

Visiting Logan Mountain

Located in the heart of the Canadian Rockies, Logan Mountain is accessible year-round though some facilities close in winter. The nearest major town is Banff (85km east) with full services.

Visitor Tips

Best time to visit: July-September for hiking, December-March for winter sports. June offers wildflowers with fewer crowds.

What to bring: Layered clothing (mountain weather changes rapidly), sturdy hiking boots, bear spray (available for rent), camera, and plenty of water.

Permits required: All visitors need a Parks Canada pass. Backcountry camping requires additional permits.

Activities & Attractions

  • Summit Trail - Challenging 14km hike to base camp with 1,500m elevation gain
  • Emerald Lake - Easy 5km loop with stunning mountain reflections
  • Glacier Skywalk - Glass-floored observation platform 280m above valley
  • Wildlife Viewing - Dawn/dusk at Logan Meadows offers best sightings
  • Interpretive Center - Exhibits on geology, ecology and Indigenous history

Nearby Destinations

  • Banff National Park (85km east) - Iconic Canadian mountain town
  • Jasper National Park (90km north) - Larger wilderness area
  • Yoho National Park (60km west) - Waterfalls and fossil beds
  • Lake Louise (65km southeast) - Famous turquoise lake
